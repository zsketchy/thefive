Principles

1/ Regard others’ well-being as you would your own
        Christianity: “Do unto others as you would have them do unto you.” (Luke 6:31)
        Islam: “None of you truly believes until he loves for his brother what he loves for himself.” (Hadith)
        Judaism: “What is hateful to you, do not do to your neighbor.” (Hillel)
        Buddhism: Compassion and reciprocity are central (e.g., Metta Sutta).
        Confucianism: “Do not impose on others what you do not wish for yourself.” (Analects 15:23)
        Philosophy: Matches Kant’s “universalizable maxim” and humanist ethics.

2/ Seek and espouse truth, even inconveniently
        Christianity: “The truth shall set you free.” (John 8:32)
        Islam: “Say: My Lord has commanded justice... and that you should set your faces (toward Him) at every place of worship.” (Qur’an 7:29) — truth is an attribute of God.
        Hinduism: Satya (truth) is one of the highest virtues in the Yamas.
        Buddhism: Right Speech includes speaking truthfully; enlightenment itself is seeing things as they are.
        Philosophy: Socratic “Know thyself”; Enlightenment rationalism; Stoic commitment to reason.

3/ To create and to give humbly is to flourish
        Christianity: Giving and serving are central (“It is more blessed to give than to receive.” Acts 20:35).
        Islam: Charity (Zakat) and humility (Tawadu‘) are duties.
        Hinduism: Karma yoga - selfless action and creative duty without attachment to reward.
        Buddhism: Compassionate giving (Dana), non-egoic contribution.
        Judaism: Tzedakah (righteous giving), humility before God.
        Philosophy: Aristotelian eudaimonia (flourishing through virtuous activity), Stoic service to humanity, modern humanist altruism.

4/ Cultivate your body, mind, and spirit
        Christianity: Stewardship of body as temple of the Spirit, mental renewal, prayer.
        Islam: Balance between physical, intellectual, and spiritual life (body as trust from God).
        Hinduism: Union of all three through yoga (literally “yoking” of body, mind, and soul).
        Buddhism: Mindfulness, meditation, moral discipline - a threefold cultivation.
        Confucianism: Self-cultivation (xiū shēn) is the root of good governance.
        Philosophy: Greeks valued arete (excellence of whole person); Stoics sought harmony between mind and nature.

5/ Be an example, now - it’s the strongest argument
        Christianity: “Let your light shine before others.” (Matthew 5:16)
        Islam: Prophet Muhammad’s life as uswah hasanah (beautiful example).
        Buddhism: The Buddha’s enlightenment and conduct as the teaching itself.
        Confucianism: Junzi (the exemplary person) teaches by virtue, not words.
        Stoicism: “Don’t explain your philosophy. Embody it.” (Epictetus)
        Humanism: Ethical authenticity and consistency as moral persuasion.


Practices

1/ One period of rest (0.5/1 day)
    Practice: Taking regular, intentional rest from labor, consumption, and distraction.
        Judaism & Christianity: Central - Sabbath (Shabbat/Sunday rest) is holy time for reflection and renewal.
        Islam: Jumu‘ah (Friday) combines communal gathering and partial rest.
        Buddhism: Uposatha days for stillness and self-reflection.
        Hinduism: Cyclical fasting and retreat days (Ekadashi, etc.).
        Confucianism & Taoism: Harmony with natural cycles; stillness as virtue.
        Stoicism: Advocates deliberate withdrawal for perspective; Seneca’s “rehearsal of leisure.”
        Modern humanism: Psychological need for rest and digital sabbath.

2/ Two nights to human connection (if partnered, 1 to them)
    Practice: Protecting time for deep, present connection with loved ones and new ones.
        Judaism/Christianity/Islam: Shared meals, family evenings, communal worship, hospitality — all sacred.
        Buddhism & Hinduism: Sangha and Seva — community and service as integral to practice.
        Confucianism: Filial piety and social ritual (li) as moral foundation.
        toicism & Aristotelian ethics: Friendship (philia) as highest good; virtue expressed in community.
        Humanism: Relationships as primary source of meaning.

3/ Three workouts per week (1 muscular/skeletal, 2 cardiovascular) 
    Practice: Individual health as a foundation for doing good.
        Hinduism: Yoga as union of body and spirit.
        Buddhism: Middle Way — health supports mindfulness.
        Islam: Physical health valued as a trust from God; Prophet encouraged fitness and moderation.
        Christianity: Body as “temple of the Spirit.”
        Judaism: Stewardship of life; care of body as mitzvah.
        Stoicism: Tempered physical training to serve virtue and endurance.
        Taoism: Qi Gong, Tai Chi — cultivating life force through movement.
        Humanism: Empirical link between movement, mood, and moral clarity.
